## Overview

Artificial Intelligence has rapidly become a common source of information, advice, and problem-solving support. From academic assistance to personal guidance, AI-generated responses are increasingly influencing how people make decisions and interpret information. However, while significant research has focused on the technical capabilities of AI systems, fewer studies have explored how people subjectively evaluate AI-generated responses when compared directly to human-written responses.

This project investigates a fundamental question: **When individuals do not know whether a response was written by an AI or a human, which answer do they prefer?**

To examine this question, our team designed and conducted a blind survey study involving 21 participants. We created 12 open-ended questions covering a wide range of topics, including leadership, personal growth, career decisions, happiness, motivation, trust, loneliness, and interpersonal conflict. For every question, participants were presented with two anonymous responses: one generated by an AI system and one written by a human. The source of each response was hidden to eliminate bias and ensure that participants evaluated answers solely based on their content.

Participants were asked to choose the response they found more helpful, convincing, meaningful, or appropriate. After collecting and analyzing the survey results, we identified clear preference patterns across different categories of questions.

The findings revealed that AI-generated responses were preferred in 8 out of 12 questions, representing an overall preference rate of 66.7%. AI responses tended to perform well in situations requiring structure, clarity, objectivity, and practical guidance. In contrast, human-written responses were more frequently preferred in emotionally sensitive contexts, particularly those involving trust, failure, comfort, and personal recovery.

One particularly interesting finding involved the topic of loneliness. Although loneliness is generally considered an emotional subject, participants still preferred the AI response. This suggests that some emotional challenges may be viewed as problems requiring actionable solutions rather than emotional validation, highlighting the complexity of human-AI communication preferences.

The results of this study suggest that response effectiveness depends less on the identity of the author and more on the context and needs of the reader. Rather than demonstrating that AI is universally superior or inferior to human communication, the study indicates that AI and humans excel in different situations and may serve complementary roles.

This repository contains the research methodology, survey design, collected data, analysis, findings, presentation materials, and future research directions developed as part of our capstone project.
